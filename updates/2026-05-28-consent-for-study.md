# Call for Crowdsourced Climbing Imagery!

This project applies an archaeological lens to one of the world's most storied mountain environments. Rather than treating the debris left on Chomolungma (Mt. Everest) solely as a pollution problem, we approach it as material evidence, a layered record of over a century of human presence, technology, ambition, and cultural practice at extreme altitude.

Using computer vision and machine learning applied to a crowdsourced photographic archive, we are mapping the nature and distribution of this material culture across routes, altitudes, and decades. The project will produce the first systematic, longitudinal archaeological study of Chomolungma as a humanly-shaped landscape.

Would you like to help? 

## What We Are Looking For 

We are seeking photographs taken during climbs on Chomolungma that capture any form of material debris or equipment encountered on the mountain, from discarded oxygen cylinders, tent remains, fixed ropes, food or fuel packaging, to abandoned gear, cairns, or any other human-made artefacts. Images from any route, altitude, era, or season are of interest. Recent digital photographs and scans of archival film images are equally welcome. Submitted photographs should not include any individuals and should focus on debris/objects only.

## Here's How!

  → Upload your photos (any format, any resolution)
  → Read the study information and check the consent box
  → Optionally tick a box to receive a one-time email when our results are published

If you have a collection of photos you might share, please visit our secure submission portal:
+ [share my old photos/videos](https://framaforms.org/archaeology-impossible-project-consensual-contribution-form-1778608928). 

OR, if you are on an expedition right now, please use this:
+ [share my current expedition photos](https://ee.kobotoolbox.org/x/93BMUDxR).

The first form uses Framaforms to handle this upload. Framaforms is a privacy-first, GDPR-compliant platform based in France. Your identity is replaced with a pseudonymous code in our dataset (unless you indicate otherwise, ie, to receive credit if you also give us permission to reproduce a photo in a publication). Your data is not shared with third parties. The second form uses Kobotoolbox and is similarly handled.

The difference between the two forms is that one can allow batch-uploading, while the other can with your permission associate your geolocation with the photograph you take right there on your expedition.

## Details & Ethics Clearance

This study is led by Dr. Shawn Graham (Carleton University) and Dr. Justin Walsh (Chapman University). 

Submitted images are associated only with a pseudonymous code in our dataset. For a subset of images, we create annotations that describe the pixel coordinates for the material culture that can be seen. This pixel-level data is then used to create an object identification model. The trained model then will be used on the larger dataset so that we can map and study the evolution over time for this material culture.  

Should you change your mind about sharing materials with us, you may withdraw those materials within six months of the initial upload date. After six months, the annotated data will have been incorporated into our object detection model. To withdraw your materials, please write to archaeology.impossible@gmail.com with your name and email and the date you uploaded materials so that we can find them and delete them. 

After six months, the annotated data will have been incorporated into our object detection model. If you choose to withdraw after six months we cannot remove the data from the model but we will commit to not discussing, describing, or publishing an image that is withdrawn.

This research has been cleared by Carleton University Research Ethics Board-B Clearance # 126216. If you have any ethical concerns with the study, please contact Carleton University Research Ethics Board, preferably by email at ethics@carleton.ca or you can leave a message by phone at 613-520-2600 ext. 2517. For all other concerns, please contact the researcher.
